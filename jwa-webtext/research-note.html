Toward a Code of Ethics for Scholarly Web Scraping in Rhetoric and Composition

Justin Lewis · companion to the Pinakes article in The Journal of Writing Analytics
Structured Abstract
AimThis research note proposes a code of ethics for the scholarly web scraping used to build bibliographic infrastructure in Rhetoric and Composition/Writing Studies. It is a companion to an article describing Pinakes, an open index of the field's scholarship; that tool could not have been built without collecting metadata from publishers' sites, and the collection raised ethical questions the field has not yet addressed in a sustained way.

It does not speak directly to the case at issue here: collecting publicly available bibliographic metadata from scholarly publishers in order to build disciplinary infrastructure. Aim

Writing analytics sits inside the larger field of Rhetoric and Composition/Writing Studies1 (CIP Code 23.13; National Center for Education Statistics, 2020) as the program of research most concerned with what computational methods can show the field about its scholarly tradition, and its community has lately taken institutional form inside the field’s flagship conference (Gresham et al., 2025). As the field develops its computational capacities, scholars are building tools that require collecting data from the open web. Citation network analyzers, disciplinary indexes, co-authorship mappers: all of these depend on metadata that is sometimes available through structured APIs and sometimes available only by visiting publisher websites systematically. I encountred this challenge directly when building Pinakes, a free open index of Writing Studies scholarship that aggregates roughly 55,000 journal articles across fifty-five journals and over 3,000 book and chapter records from CrossRef, OpenAlex, RSS feeds, lighteweight scrapers, and hand-built bibliographic data, as of July 2026. A companion article in this volume describes the tool, document’s it’s metadata sources, its coverage and its blind spots, the analytical features it supports, and the Right to Erasure mechanism by which authors may remove their names from the index, and it argues that the unevenness of the field’s metadata infrastructure is itself a finding. This note extends the work of that article, exploring what researchers owe, ethically, to the publishers and authors whose metadata makes such an index possible and sketches a series of best practices and principles that should guide automated data collection in Writing studies.

The ethical questions this work raised were largely unaddressed by the frameworks available in the existing literature from our field. The Association of Internet Researchers has produced three iterations of ethical guidelines for internet research (Ess & the Association of Internet Researchers, 2002; Franzke et al., 2020), and Brown et al. (2025) recently proposed a comprehensive framework for web scraping in social science research that addresses legal, ethical, institutional, and scientific dimensions. Writing from within Writing Studies, McKee and Porter (2009) argued that digital research ethics should attend to the specific rhetorical situation of each case rather than applying blanket rules. What none of these frameworks address with specificity, however, is the ethical terrain appropriate to a particular kind of scraping: the collection of publicly available bibliographic metadata from often independent and small-run (sometimes open-access) scholarly publishers for the purpose of building disciplinary infrastructure tools.

Discipline-specific infrastructure like Pinakes benefits the field broadly, from graduate students mapping a scholarly conversation to scholars running down citationality across disparate sources to programs accounting for their scholarly output, but I’d argue it benefits most the readers and writers who are least served by the general-purpose commercial tools that were not built with our field as a guiding North Star. Yet, the labor to build scholarly metadata infrastructure is not evenly distributed. As an instructor at a two-year college without meaningful research or release support, the practices of vibe-coding and the principles I outline below are built with someone like me in mind. An ethics of scholarly infrastructure that ignored my own positionality and the academic asymmetry wouldn’t do service to the tactical choices I’ve made to try and address a problem I’ve encountered since graduate school; namely, the lack of a well-developed, usable, pleasurable method for visualizing the deep networks and distant views that constitute our disciplinary epistemic centers and peripheries.

In that spirit, this note proposes nine guiding principles for that activity. I offer them as a first-pass framework, one that I hope colleagues in Writing Studies and in writing analytics will critique, refine, and extend. The field needs this conversation, and at present my experience suggests that we lack discipline-specific guidelines that address it.

Problem Formation

The distinction between bibliographic metadata scraping and human-subjects data scraping is important because the ethical terrain is materially different. Franzke et al. (2020) and Brown et al. (2025) are primarily concerned with scraping data produced by human subjects: social media posts, forum discussions, user-generated content, contexts where questions of consent, privacy, and the public/private boundary are front and center. Scraping a publisher’s catalog page to extract book titles, author names, ISBNs, and series information involves none of those concerns. There are no human subjects. There is no personally identifiable information beyond what the authors themselves published under their own names. The data is bibliographic metadata that the publisher has made publicly available on the open web.

The relevant questions that follow: May a scholar collect metadata from a publisher’s site when the publisher has not explicitly permitted automated access, but has not forbidden it either? How fast may a scraper request pages before it begins to burden a servet that a small press or a volunteer-run journal maintains on thin to non-existent budget? When a publisher’s records are inconsistent or wrong, what does a scholar owe to the accuracy of the index that’s built from them? And, finally, once metadata has been collected and made searchable, what continuing obligation does the scholar have to authors who would prefer their work not appear? These are the questions that the principles below are intended to answer.

McKee and Porter’s (2009) insistence on rhetorical situation is useful here; as they argue, the ethical framework appropriate to any digital research activity should derive from the particularities of that activity’s rhetorical context. The rhetorical situation of scraping the Southern Illinois University Press catalog is materially different from the situation of scraping a social media platform. The data consists of publicly listed bibliographic records rather than user-generated content; the stakeholders are publishers and authors rather than research subjects; the potential for harm is constrained to server load and representational accuracy rather than privacy violation; and the purpose is disciplinary infrastructure rather than behavioral analysis.

The field of Writing Studies is not without ethical reflection on its own data. Writing analytics has taken up de-identification in the handling of student writing (Rudniy, 2018) and the ethics of analytic methods more broadly (Knight et al., 2023); Phelps (2021) has built an extended account of research ethics in Writing Studies around the federal Common Rule; and The Journal of Writing Assessment's 2016 special issue developed a theory of ethics for assessment (Elliot, 2016). The conversation continues in the present volume, where Toth et al. (2026) work through principled decision making for generative AI in writing placement, and the journal community has begun taking stock of writing analytics as a program of research (Gresham et al., 2025). What this literature has not yet addressed, as far as I am aware, is the specific case of collecting, structuring, and circulating bibliographic metadata about the field's published record, which is the case this note takes up.

As computational methods become more common and as projects that make disciplinary knowledge infrastructure visible multiply, the absence of an ethical framework specific to this kind of work becomes harder to ignore Drawing on the foundational study of classification systems in Sorting Things Out, Bowker and Star (1999) argued that infrastructure is never neutral; the categories and connections that get built, or that remain unbuilt, shape what can be known and by whom. Nor is that insight only a science-studies one. Writing Studies has developed its own line of work on the infrastructures that underwrite research and publication in the field (Read, 2019; Frith, 2020; Read & Frith, 2022; Adams, 2022). The argument I make here extends both traditions to the ethics of building the infrastructure itself: to the choices made in the data acquisition and cleaning phases, before any output exists. How we collect, what we leave out, and whose labor makes the tool possible are ethical questions that precede anything the finished index can show. The claim that infrastructure is never neutral is not only a science studies insight. Writing Studies scholarship has developed its own line of work on the infrastructures that underwrite research and publication in the field (Read, 2019; Frith, 2020; Read & Frith, 2022; Adams, 2022). The present argument I present extends the ethics of building the infrastructure itself to the choices made before any infrastructural object exists in the data acquisition and cleaning phases that undergird such labor.

Methods and/or Discussion

The framework I work from is the Association of Internet Researcher’s Internet Research: Ethical Guidelines 3.0 (Franzke et al., 2020). AoIR is the principle professional body for internet research across disciplines, and its guidelines, now in a third version unanimously approved by the membership in 2019, are the most widely used reference for thinking through the ethics of studying online materials. Rather than issue fixed rules, IRE 3.0 proceeds case by case, asking researchers to reason from context about the people and communities applied in a given community. McKee and Porter’s (2009) explicitly rhetorical, case-based approach resonates with that orientation and will feel familiar to scholars in Writing Studies. Both sources are appropriate for considering the current problem because scraping bibliographic metadata is not one activity with one ethical stance but a range of human and machinic assemblages whose stakes depend on what is collected and how.

Three commitments from the AoIR framework shape how I think the principles that follow should be read. First, IRE 3.0 insists on ethical pluralism and cross-cultural awareness; it refuses to offer a single ethical framework or a universal checklist and instead asks researchers to develop what it calls “guidelines, not recipes” (Ess & the Association of Internet Researchers, 2002, p. 3). Second, and relatedly, it treats ethics as inseparable from method. Annette Markham’s formulation that “ethics is method; method is ethics” (2006) runs through all three iterations of the AoIR documents and aligns with how our field already thinks about the relationship between research design and research responsibility. Third, IRE 3.0 emphasizes that ethical reflection is an ongoing process rather than a one-time approval exercise. As the document notes, the research landscape “continues to change and transform, often in dramatic ways, over a very short period of time” (Franzke et al., 2020, p. 4); as such, ethical attention must persist across every stage of a project, from initial design through data collection, analysis, storage, and dissemination.

Scraping has a small methodological literature in the field’s technical communication wing already: Gallagher and Beveridge (2021) frame project-oriented web scraping as a research practice with its own design and ethical considerations. The principles I sketch below extend that conversation from data about discourse to data about the field’s own bibliographic record.

What follows is a set of nine guiding principles derived from the practices I developed while building Pinakes. I hold the AoIR’s process orientation in view while offering something concrete enough to be actionable. These ethical principles are more than a numbered list that could be mistaken for a safety checksheet of the sort that engineering codes provide. Rather, they are normative rather than procedural and non-teleological: they do not calculate the consequences of scraping and weigh harms against benefits, and they don’t aim at a single, tidy resolution that would settle every case in advance. Instead, they describe a set of commitments and ask the scholar to reason from them towards the particular research context at hand.

This orientation is part of a long tradition, one that Rawls (1999, 2001) develops in which justice is approached through principles that hold generally but are always responsive to local conditions. The Association of Internet Researchers’ guidelines work in much the same spirit, drawing on basic ethical principles and on the ethical pluralism that the Belmont Report (1979) and the Common Rule provide for institutions that oversee research practice. The goal is not to resolve all research problems. Instead, the aim is to give the field a shared place to begin reflecting and reasoning about research practices that it has taken up far faster than it has discussed.

In the sections that follow, each principle is situated within the AoIR’s broader ethical commitments and illustrated through the specific decisions I made during the construction of Pinakes.

1. Respect publishers’ stated preferences about automated access.

When they set out to scrape, scholars in Writing Studies should respect the preferences publishers state about automated access.

The robots.txt file is the publisher’s stated preference about what automated agents may and may not access. Compliance is non-negotiable and builders should recognize that a desire for completeness does not override consent. This principle has a clear analog in the AoIR guidelines’ attention to terms of service and platform-specific directives. As IRE 3.0 notes, “various research activities might violate the terms and conditions of online platforms,” and researchers must grapple with the fact that ToS agreements constrain what data collection is permissible regardless of the researcher’s intent (Franzke et al., 2020, p. 14). In this way, the robots.txt file functions as a lightweight version of that constraint: the publisher telling you, in machine-readable terms, what they consent to.

2. Rate-limit requests so that collection does not burden the infrastructure that hosts scholarship.

When they configure a scraper, scholars in Writing Studies should rate-limit requests so that collection does not strain the servers, often small and volunteer-funded, that host the field’s scholarship.

The AoIR’s attention to “power and power imbalances” (Franzke et al., 2020, p. 12) finds a concrete application in the relationship between an automated scraper and a small publisher’s web server. That relationship is inherently asymmetric, and the ethical obligation falls on the party with more technical capacity; as such, this principle constitutes a practical expression of what the AoIR framework describes as beneficence: the obligation to minimize harm to research subjects and, just as importantly, to the broader infrastructure that supports them.

Small scholarly publishers often run on modest hosting. Flooding a server with rapid automated requests can degrade performance for human users or trigger defensive shutdowns. When a press like Parlor Press or Southern Illinois University Press hosts its catalog on institutional servers, aggressive automated access imposes a cost on every other user of that infrastructure. Pinakes scrapers wait a minimum of five seconds between requests, and longer for sites with visibly constrained resources. In other words, the scraper should be the least disruptive visitor to the site, functioning as the quietest patron in the reading room rather than the one pulling everything off the shelves.

3. Collect only publicly available bibliographic metadata.

When they engage in web scraping, scholars in Writing Studies should collect only publicly available bibliographic metadata.

No full text. No paywalled content. No content behind authentication. The scraper collects what a person would find by clicking through a publisher’s public-facing catalog pages: titles, authors, dates, series names, ISBNs, abstracts where displayed, and DOIs where available. If the data is not visible to an unauthenticated browser, it is not collected.

This principle aligns with what Borgman (2007) described as the distinction between the content layer and the metadata layer of scholarly infrastructure: responsible scraping operates exclusively at the metadata layer. It also resonates with the AoIR’s attention to privacy as contextual integrity, drawing on Nissenbaum’s (2009) account of informational norms. The context is really important: bibliographic metadata published on a catalog page exists in a public information flow that the publisher has chosen to make visible. Scraping it does not alter the informational context in the way that, say, collecting social media data from a semi-private group would. I argue that the field needs to name this distinction explicitly if it is going to develop ethical guidelines that fit the actual work of bibliometric infrastructure building rather than borrowing wholesale from frameworks designed for Big Data scenarios with fundamentally different stakes.

4. Limit collection to the metadata, and treat the full-text of the scholarship as out-of-bounds.

When they decide what to gather, scholars in Writing Studies should limit collection to bibliographic metadata and treat the scholarship itself, the articles and chapters and monographs, as out of bounds.

This is the distinction between metadata and the scholarship itself. Pinakes does not host, reproduce, or redistribute any copyrighted content. It indexes; it does not replicate. The index entry points users toward the original source.

I want to stress this because the field’s relationship with commercial publishers is already fraught. The temptation to work around restrictions in order to achieve more comprehensive coverage is real; yet, doing so would undermine the integrity of the project and, I would argue, the field’s ability to make credible claims about the importance of open infrastructure. The AoIR guidelines are direct on this point: “Even outbound browsing of web pages can potentially be dangerous, for example, if it invites retaliation” (Franzke et al., 2020, Appendix 7.1, p.79). Scholars building infrastructure tools operate in a space where publishers have lawyers and where the line between metadata collection and content extraction must remain unambiguous. To that end, I contend that the distinction between indexing and reproducing is one that infrastructure builders must hold with particular care.

5. Make the purpose and method of collection clear to the field.

When they build and report a scraping project, scholars in Writing Studies should make its purpose and method clear, so that others can see what was collected and why, and replicate that process if useful.

Brown et al. (2025) emphasized the importance of transparency in data collection methods, and their framework provides useful orientation in this use context. The Coverage page on Pinakes documents which journals and publishers were scraped, what data was collected, and what methods were used. Anyone whose data appears in the index can see how it got there. When feasible, I would encourage contacting publishers directly to inform them that their catalog metadata is being indexed; however, I recognize that this is not always practical for a solo practitioner working across dozens of sources.

The AoIR framework treats transparency as foundational across all three iterations of its guidelines. IRE 3.0 articulates this commitment through what it calls “a general structure for ethical analysis” that asks researchers to consider how they communicate their collection to affected parties (Franzke et al., 2020, pp. 12-22). For a project like Pinakes, legibility means more than a methods section in a journal article; it means building transparency into the tool itself: documenting coverage gaps, explaining why some journals have richer data than others, making visible the structural asymmetries in metadata infrastructure that determine what the index can and cannot do, etc.

Markham's “impact model” for ethical assessment, included as a companion resource in IRE 3.0, offers a useful framework for thinking through these consequences at multiple levels of scale. She identifies four arenas of potential impact, including the treatment of individual people, side effects of research, downstream data use, and what she describes as the future-oriented implications of research design (Markham, 2018). For bibliographic infrastructure work, the most relevant arenas are the latter two: the ways that aggregated metadata, once structured and made searchable, can shape who gets seen and who gets overlooked; as well as the long-term implications of building discovery tools that will influence how researchers encounter the field's literature. These are consequences that deserve transparent accounting, and they point toward questions that future work on Writing Studies infrastructure ethics will need to address more directly.

6. Accept the limits that access norms impose, rather than circumventing them.

When they meet a barrier to access, scholars in Writing Studies should accept the limits those norms impose rather than engineer circumventions.

The Pinakes monograph index has significant holes. Certain journal archives resist automated extraction, and some publishers’ websites are structured in ways that make comprehensive scraping impractical without circumventing access norms. I left those holes in place. Misrepresenting coverage by working around restrictions would undermine the integrity of the tool, and the gaps themselves turned out to be informative: the difficulty of indexing certain commercial publishers’ catalogs reveals something about the relationship between commercial publishing and open metadata infrastructure that would have stayed invisible if I had found a workaround. In this way, the absences in the index carry their own analytical weight.

The AoIR guidelines frame this commitment in terms of “data minimization,” a principle drawn from the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation that holds that researchers should only retrieve the data needed to pursue a specific research question (Ess & Hård af Segerstad, 2019). For bibliographic infrastructure projects, the analog is what I would call scope discipline: resisting the urge to collect everything you technically can collect and instead limiting collection to what you can collect ethically and accurately. When a publisher’s website structure, terms of service, or robots.txt makes certain data inaccessible, the responsible choice is to document the gap in the build notes and let the limitation stand. Those documented absences become part of the project’s methodological record, available to future researchers who may find different or better approaches.

7. Design scrapers to behave like a careful human reader moving through a site.

When they decide to write code, scholars in Writing Studies should design their scrapers to behave like a careful human reader moving through a site at a human pace.

The comparison to human pace in this recommendation underscores that scraping should feel like manual work inasmuch as it proceeds slowly, with pauses, during ordinary hours, creating a natural ceiling on the load placed on any given source. Principle seven respects both the stated preferences of the publisher and their infrastructural capacity, and it has useful resonance with the AoIR's emphasis on contextual integrity: the ethical question goes beyond whether data is technically public, since what matters equally is whether the manner of collection respects the norms of the informational context in which that data circulates. A catalog page exists to be browsed, and a scraper that browses it methodically, at a human-like pace, is operating within those norms. A scraping practice can collect nothing but metadata and still be doing something wrong if it acquires that metadata in a way that ignores access norms or overwhelms a server. When a scraper would do something a human sitting behind an internet connection plainly could not, hammering a server with thousands of requests per minute, around the clock, extracting from hidden endpoints, this principle has been violated.

8. Store and present collected metadata accurately, and document every normalization.

When they clean and publish the data, scholars in Writing Studies should store and present it accurately, documenting every change for norming purposes on the records as they found them.

The threshold for what counts as “accurate” in this context means more than just not inventing data. It means representing the source as it actually is, including where the source if flawed. Bibliographic metadata is frequently inconsistent due to multiple factors in the digitization process. It isn’t uncommon to find the same author appear with three different spellings across multiple publishers or a chapter’s publication year disagreeing with a volume’s – if you’re researched long in our field, you, too, have likely encountered these kinds of corrupted artifacts. The scraper that silently “fixes” these without a human in the loop makes an editorial decision masquerading as a technical one. Taken in aggregate and across a large dataset like the one that powers Pinakes creates an index that misrepresents the very scholarly tradition(s) that it claims to describe. As such, when working with metadata, a researcher’s responsibility is two-fold: first, they must carry the source’s data through faithfully, and, when cleaning is genuinely necessary, to clean transparently and document the changes made. Brown et al. (2025) make a similar point that a scraped dataset is a constructed artifact whose construction must be reported so that downstream users are able to interpret it correctly. For bibliographic indexes like Pinakes, reporting lets users differentiate in the tool results genuine gaps in the field’s citation practices or simply gaps in the metadata itself. If an index faithfully records an error that is visible in it’s original form, it is doing its job; however, if an index propagates an error silently without notification though normalization has occurred, it is not representing ethically.

With all this in mind, researchers should not alter, editorialize, or selectively represent the metadata they collect. Author names should appear as the publisher listed them. Publication dates should reflect what the source reports. Where OCR artifacts, encoding errors, or inconsistencies appear in scraped data, clean the data transparently and document the cleaning process. In the case of Pinakes, I’ve developed a bespoke CrossRef metadata cleaning tool2 for just such a purpose.

The AoIR’s companion resource on AI and machine learning (Bechmann & Zevenbergen, 2020) includes an extended discussion of data cleaning that applies directly here. Noting that “the time spent on cleaning a dataset and the assumptions that go into this process should be communicated more clearly in the resulting research,” Bechmann and Zevenbergen argue that cleaning choices can “normalize data” in ways that carry consequences for representation (p. 41). For a bibliographic index, every normalization decision embeds assumptions that affect what the index shows its users: how to handle variant author names, how to reconcile conflicting publication dates, how to tag topics computationally, and so on. Documenting those decisions is not a methodological nicety but, rather, I’d argue that it is an ethical obligation.

9. Remain responsive to removal requests for as long as the tool operates.

When they maintain a live index, scholars in Writing Studies should remain responsive to requests for removal for as long as the tool is available.

If a publisher or author requests that their metadata be removed from the index, comply. The purpose of a scholarly index is to serve the community, not to override individual preferences. The “Right to Erasure” tool on Pinakes honors this principle’s intent.

The AoIR guidelines situate this under a broader discussion of what they call “post-research data governance” (Bechmann & Zevenbergen, 2020, p. 45). For a living index rather than a completed research project, the obligation is ongoing: the tool must remain responsive to the community it serves for as long as it operates. I would note, however, that this principle sits in some tension with the goal of comprehensive coverage. An author who requests removal of their metadata is exercising a legitimate preference; yet their removal changes the index’s representation of the field. I have not resolved this tension, and I suspect other infrastructure builders will encounter it too. The principle as stated privileges individual autonomy over completeness, and I think that ordering is correct, though other scholars working in this space may arrive at a different calculus.

Conclusions

These nine principles are partial. They address one kind of activity, and we should expect that other situations in computational Writing Studies research will raise ethical questions these principles cannot anticipate. They are specific to the scraping of bibliographic metadata from scholarly publishers, and they should be understood as a contribution to a conversation the field is just beginning to explore.

The AoIR’s process-oriented framework reminds me that these principles need to remain open to revision. As the guidelines note, “ambiguity, uncertainty, and disagreement are inevitable” in ethical reflection (Ess & the Association of Internet Researchers, 2002, pp. 3-4). I am persuaded that the right response to that inevitability is direct engagement: articulating principles, testing them in practice, documenting what works, and inviting critique. Inasmuch as the AoIR’s own documents have gone through three major iterations since 2002, the expectation that any first-pass framework would need revision seems built into the enterprise.

Several tensions within the framework deserve further attention. For example principles 6 and 9 seem, at first blush, antithetical. A bibliographic index that allows publishers to opt out of being indexed could develop systematic blind spots that distort the field’s self-understanding; yet the alternative, insisting on indexing over objection, seems difficult to defend for a tool whose purpose is to serve the scholarly community, especially in the context of our current political climate where research agendas are often weaponized and used to ruin scholarly careers. I have chosen to privilege autonomy over completeness, but I recognize that this choice carries consequences.

The question of what obligations a scraper owes to a publisher that has chosen not to invest in open metadata infrastructure is similarly complex. Principle 6 asks the scraper to accept coverage gaps rather than work around restrictions. In the case of Pinakes, this meant accepting a Routledge catalog that was 73 percent incomplete, because the company’s sparse CrossRef metadata and website structure made comprehensive automated collection impractical without circumventing access norms. That acceptance is ethically sound; however, it produces an index that structurally underrepresents one of the field’s largest commercial puiblishers, which has consequences for the index’s analytical uses. It is important to acknowledge this gap and recognize the limitation of the tool based on this omission.

Directions for Further Action

I would encourage the field’s professional organizations, editorial boards, and graduate programs to develop this conversation further, drawing on the substantial work already done by the Association of Internet Researchers (Franzke et al., 2020), by McKee and Porter (2009) in their rhetorical approach to internet research ethics, by Brown et al. (2025) on the legal and ethical dimensions of web scraping, and by D’Ignazio and Klein (2020) in their articulation of data feminism’s commitments to transparency and accountability. The field’s own existing conversations about citational justice, about the politics of scholarly infrastructure, about who builds the tools and who gets indexed, provide the disciplinary grounding that generic internet research ethics guidelines inevitably lack.

What I am suggesting is that as our scholarship becomes more computational and our tools more data-dependent, extending our ethical attention to our methods of data collection seems both natural and overdue.

Extending ethical attention to our methods of data collection is continuous with commitments the field already holds. Writing Studies has long asked researchers to represent the people they study fairly and to cite the work of others with care; treating the metadata of scholarship, and the labor and infrastructure behind that metadata, as something owed accuracy and restraint applies those same commitments to a new object. The temporal lag between how we govern this kind of work and the development of the technologies that facilitate it is an instance of what Stiegler (2009, p. 2) describes as techno-genesis structurally preceding socio-genesis: the technical practice arrives first, and under conditions of permanent innovation that precedence becomes a matter of speed, with the rhythms of computational method and data acquisition outrunning the ethical conversations of the field employing them.

Several concrete steps would advance this work. A working group within one of the field’s professional organizations (CWPA, CCCC, or the Council for Programs in Technical and Scientific Communication) could take up the task of drafting discipline-specific guidelines, building on the framework proposed here and on the AoIR’s existing resources. Graduate programs that are incorporating computational methods into their curricula could include ethical data collection as a component of methods training. Journal editors and editorial boards, particularly those considering the adoption of bibliometric tools or data-driven review practices, could articulate their own expectations about how the field’s scholarship should be collected, structured, and circulated by third-party tools.

I’m confident Writing Studies has the intellectual resources to do this well. The field’s long engagement with the ethics of writing, with questions of access and power in knowledge production, with the politics of who gets cited and who gets read, positions it to think carefully about the ethical dimensions of the tools its members build to make scholarship visible. I hope these principles serve as a starting point for that work; yet the real progress will come from the colleagues who take them up, test them against their own projects, and find their limits.
