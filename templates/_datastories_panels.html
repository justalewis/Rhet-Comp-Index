Citation flow between Technical & Professional Communication and Rhetoric/Composition, decade by decade. Each Sankey panel shows where citations originate (left) and where they land (right). The decade summary table beneath quantifies what the eye picks up.

Methodology

Tool orientation

The Braided Path tracks citation traffic between Technical & Professional Communication (TPC) and Rhetoric/Composition (RC) decade by decade. Each Sankey panel shows where the decade's citations originate (left) and where they land (right); width encodes count. The decade summary table beneath quantifies what the eye picks up.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the diagram traces a striking growth and asymmetry. Pre-1990s the field carried 901 inter- and intra-cluster citations — 258 TPC→TPC, 462 RC→RC, 79 TPC→RC, and only 9 RC→TPC. By the 2020s the total has grown twentyfold to 18,250, and the asymmetry persists: TPC cites RC at 862 edges per decade while RC cites TPC at 444. TPC remains the more outwardly engaged neighborhood; RC stays largely inside its own conversation across all five decades.

Methodology

Each citation edge is bucketed by the decade of its citing article. Source and target articles are mapped to a field group via the journal_groups classification (TPC / RC / OTHER). Within each decade, citations are aggregated to TPC→TPC, TPC→RC, RC→TPC, RC→RC, plus OTHER as a residual category. Sankey nodes are per-decade group buckets; widths are raw citation counts. The journal_groups mapping is hand-curated and remains stable across runs.1

Controls

Cluster, journals, and year range apply to both the citing and cited side. Selecting Composition & Writing Studies draws the Sankey using only edges where both endpoints are in CWS journals — useful for asking what intra-cluster citation traffic looks like. Narrowing the year range compares a specific decade against the rest. The decade selector switches which decade pane the diagram displays.

References

  1. Sankey, M. H. P. R. (1898). Introductory note on the thermal efficiency of steam-engines. Minutes of Proceedings of the Institution of Civil Engineers, 134, 278–283.

How the corpus is composed: every indexed journal sorted by tradition. The classification is fixed (the same one all the cross-field tools rely on); this panel shows the resulting balance of articles per group and per journal.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Branching Traditions is the corpus snapshot every cross-field tool depends on. It sorts every indexed journal into one of three traditions and reports the resulting balance of journals and articles per group. Read it as the answer to "what is this index actually composed of?"

Run with default parameters across the full indexed corpus, three traditions emerge. Rhetoric & Composition is the largest at 24 journals and 33,131 articles — 64% of the indexed corpus. Technical & Professional Communication is concentrated and prolific: 9 journals carrying 8,223 articles. The Other / Adjacent bucket — writing centers, WAC, multimodal, international, community-engaged venues — gathers 18 journals and 10,489 articles. The asymmetry between groups (RC has nearly four times as many articles as TPC across more than twice as many journals) is the structural fact behind every cross-cluster citation pattern in the rest of the chapters.

Methodology

The grouping (TPC / RHET_COMP / OTHER) is hand-curated and lives in journal_groups.py. OTHER covers venues whose work is rhetorically and pedagogically adjacent to writing studies but whose primary disciplinary home is elsewhere. Counts come from a single SQL aggregation over the articles table, joined to the journal-group mapping.

Controls

Year range changes which articles get counted toward the per-tradition totals. Narrowing to pre-2000 reads how the index was composed in the field's earlier era; opening to all years gives the corpus snapshot above. The cluster and journal filters are intentionally hidden because this tool is the composition snapshot.

Sources are articles cited inside the corpus but never citing anything in it (foundational works the field reaches back to). Sinks are the reverse: articles that cite into the corpus but are never cited from it (the active research frontier, plus genuine dead-ends).

Methodology

Tool orientation

Origins and Frontiers separates the indexed corpus into the work the field reaches back to (sources: cited but never citing inside the index) and the work that is currently citing into the field but has not yet been cited from it (sinks: citing but never cited). Sinks split into two sub-categories: frontier (recent enough that not being cited is normal) and data-gap (old enough that the absence of citation more likely reflects missing reference deposits than genuine non-citation).

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the tool surfaces 51,843 articles. 41,444 (79.9%) are sources — foundational work that other indexed articles cite but that itself cites nothing inside the corpus, often because its own reference list reaches into books, dissertations, or journals outside the index. 2,998 articles (5.8%) are frontier sinks under the 2021 cutoff, and 5,277 (10.2%) are data-gap sinks — likely victims of reference-deposit coverage rather than genuine isolation. The most-cited source in the field is Adrienne Rich's 1972 "When We Dead Awaken: Writing as Re-Vision" with 402 CrossRef citations — an external-influence text that the indexed corpus reaches back to without the corpus indexing what Rich herself was reading.

Methodology

Sources are articles with internal in-degree ≥ 1 and out-degree = 0; sinks are out-degree ≥ 1 and in-degree = 0. Sinks published after the frontier-cutoff year are flagged frontier; older sinks are data-gap. Per-journal rates report each venue's source-rate and sink-rate, which together describe how the venue contributes to the corpus's intellectual flow.1

Controls

Filtering to a cluster shows what is foundational for that subfield specifically — selecting Technical Communication reveals which articles the TC sub-area reaches back to. The year range shifts the frontier-cutoff implicitly: an upper bound near the present makes more articles look like frontier sinks; a tighter window makes the data-gap category more obvious.

References

  1. Hummon, N. P., & Doreian, P. (1989). Connectivity in a citation network: The development of DNA theory. Social Networks, 11(1), 39–63.

A separate Hummon-Doreian main path computed for each of five decade windows (1970s–80s, 1990s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s). Articles that appear on multiple decades' paths are flagged as persistent; articles that anchor a single decade are decade-specific.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Shifting Currents computes a separate Hummon-Doreian main path for each of five decade windows (1970s–80s, 1990s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s) and reports each decade's backbone alongside a cross-decade persistence ranking. Articles that appear on multiple decades' paths are flagged as persistent; articles that anchor a single decade are decade-specific.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the five decade paths surface different anchor articles. The 1970s–80s path is short (4 articles) and led by Mike Rose's 1980 "Rigid Rules, Inflexible Plans, and the Stifling of Language." The 1990s extends to 8 articles led by Nancy Nelson Spivey's "Transforming Texts." The 2000s shrinks back to 6 articles anchored by Dorothy Winsor's "Ordering Work." The 2010s explodes to 12 articles led by the multi-author "Symposium: On the Framework for Success in Postsecondary Writing." The 2020s holds 9 articles. The persistence list collects 39 articles that appear on more than one decade's path — a small set whose membership marks the texts that have shaped the field's backbone across multiple eras rather than within a single decade.

Methodology

For each decade, the citation DAG restricted to that decade's articles and their citations is constructed; cycles are removed via topological-sort breaks; SPC (search path count) is computed for every edge; the highest-SPC chain from a source to a sink is reported as the decade's main path.1 Cross-decade persistence is then computed by counting how many decade paths each article appears on. The result is a per-decade reading list plus a persistence ranking that surfaces cross-era anchor texts.

Controls

Filters narrow the DAG that each decade's main path is computed on. Cluster turns the tool into "the decade-by-decade backbone within this subfield." A tight year range makes the per-decade slices sparser; widening usually produces longer paths. Heavy compute on the first cache miss; identical filter combinations return in under a second after.

References

  1. Liu, J. S., & Lu, L. Y. Y. (2012). An integrated approach for main path analysis: Development of the Hirsch index as an example. Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology, 63(3), 528–542.

How long it takes citations to land, broken down by direction. TPC→RC is typically slower than TPC→TPC; this panel quantifies that and tracks how delay distributions have shifted across decades.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Speed of Influence quantifies how long it takes citations to land, broken down by source-target field group. Each citation's delay is computed as (citing year − cited year), and the resulting distributions are compared across direction with Mann-Whitney U tests. The question the tool answers is whether different cross-cluster citation directions move at materially different speeds.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the four directions show distinct tempos. TPC→TPC citations have a median delay of 6 years across 16,102 edges, the fastest direction in the corpus. RC→RC and RC→TPC are both at median 7 years (16,040 and 839 edges respectively). TPC→RC is the slowest at median 8 years across 2,915 edges. The Mann-Whitney comparison between TPC→RC and RC→TPC returns p ≈ 3 × 10−6, indicating a statistically clear delay asymmetry: when TPC reaches across to cite RC, it cites materially older work than when RC reaches across to cite TPC. The TPC→TPC vs. TPC→RC comparison is even stronger (p ≈ 9 × 10−62): citation traffic stays fastest when it stays inside the source cluster.

Methodology

Each citation edge's delay is (citing year − cited year). Edges are bucketed by the field-group of source and target into the four directional categories. Mann-Whitney U tests compare distributions pairwise to flag whether observed differences are statistically distinguishable from random fluctuation.1 Reported statistics include count, mean, median, Q25, Q75, standard deviation, and maximum.

Controls

Filters scope every direction's delay distribution. Applying a cluster filter asks how fast that subfield consumes its own work versus reaches across the boundary. The year range filters by citing year, so narrowing to a recent slice tracks contemporary citation tempo specifically — a useful diagnostic for whether cross-cluster delays are rising or falling over time.

References

  1. Mann, H. B., & Whitney, D. R. (1947). On a test of whether one of two random variables is stochastically larger than the other. Annals of Mathematical Statistics, 18(1), 50–60.

Delay over time

High-betweenness articles that connect TPC and RC neighbourhoods. These are the works that, if removed, would lengthen the average shortest path between the two sub-fields most.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Border Crossers identifies high-betweenness articles that connect TPC and RC neighborhoods. These are the works that, if removed, would lengthen the average shortest path between the two sub-fields most. Border Crossers reports both betweenness scores and a separate count of cross-cluster boundary edges incident to each candidate, so a node ranks high only if it both lies on many shortest paths and actually carries cross-boundary traffic rather than just within-cluster traffic.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus (13,442 articles, 44,322 edges), the top bridge is Jenny Edbauer's 2005 "Unframing Models of Public Distribution: From Rhetorical Situation to Rhetorical Ecologies" (RC, 135 citations, betweenness 0.034, 55 boundary edges), followed by Bruffee's 1984 "Collaborative Learning and the 'Conversation of Mankind'" (RC, 108 citations, 61 boundary edges) and Russell's 1997 "Rethinking Genre in School and Society" (RC, 129 citations, 93 boundary edges) — the article with the highest count of TPC-RC boundary edges in the entire corpus. Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" appears at fourth place but with only 16 boundary edges, indicating that its high citation count and within-cluster centrality do not translate to a heavy cross-cluster connector role: it is central to TPC, not bridging TPC to RC.

Methodology

NetworkX's exact betweenness centrality is computed on the full undirected citation graph using Brandes's algorithm.1 Boundary-crossing flow (the count of cross-cluster edges incident to each node) is reported alongside betweenness so the user can distinguish genuine bridges from locally-central nodes. The neighborhood subgraph for each top bridge is included for the force-directed view, which renders the bridge node together with its first-degree cross-cluster neighbors.

Controls

Bridge detection runs on whatever subgraph the filters carve out. Filtering to a cluster finds within-cluster bridges — useful when "TPC-vs-RC" is not the boundary of interest. Year range on citing date constrains which edges the betweenness algorithm sees. Heavy first-time compute (60–90s) per unique filter combination; cached after.

References

  1. Brandes, U. (2001). A faster algorithm for betweenness centrality. Journal of Mathematical Sociology, 25(2), 163–177.

Bridge neighbourhood

When TPC cites RC, does RC cite back? The reciprocity rate at the article level reveals how much intellectual traffic actually runs both ways and which TPC articles attract the most return citation.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Two-Way Street asks whether cross-cluster citation runs both ways: when a TPC article cites RC, does any RC article cite back? The reciprocity rate at the article level reveals how much intellectual traffic actually runs both ways and which articles attract the most return citation.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, RC reciprocates with TPC at a substantially higher rate than the reverse. Of 1,277 TPC articles that cite RC work, only 270 (21.1%) get cited back by RC; of 387 RC articles that cite TPC work, 143 (37.0%) get cited back by TPC. The asymmetry runs against intuition: even though TPC reaches out to cite RC more often than the reverse (1,277 outgoing vs. 387), RC is the more reliably reciprocating cluster when it does reach out. At the strict edge level (A→B paired with a literal B→A counterpart), the rate is 0.0% because article-pair reciprocity through the same two articles is rare; the analytic move is at the article-level reciprocity, where the question is whether each cluster's outreach gets any return engagement at all.

Methodology

Article-level reciprocity for an article A is the count of distinct articles in the other group that A both cites and is cited by. Edge-level reciprocity is the fraction of A→B edges with a B→A counterpart on the same article pair. Trends are computed per decade of the citing article so the user can read whether reciprocity rates are rising or falling over time.1

Controls

Cluster and journal filters scope which articles get counted on each side of the boundary. A useful pattern is to lock to Rhetoric, run, then switch to Technical Communication — the comparison is whether one cluster reciprocates more than the other. Year-range filters narrow the analysis to a specific decade, which can surface generational shifts in cross-cluster engagement.

References

  1. Wasserman, S., & Faust, K. (1994). Social network analysis: Methods and applications. Cambridge University Press.

Most-reciprocated articles

The Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient for the corpus's citation distribution, plus a power-law fit. The visual answer to "how unequal is citation in this field?"

Methodology

Tool orientation

Shape of Influence reports the Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient for the corpus's internal citation distribution, plus a power-law fit on the long tail. The visual answer to "how unequal is citation in this field?" lives here.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the corpus-wide Gini sits at 0.909 — a near-maximum-inequality value. Of 51,843 indexed articles, 41,444 (79.9%) have zero internal citations; the top 1% of articles capture roughly 28% of all internal citations and the top 100 articles capture 11.2%. The shape is Lotkaesque1: a small head of heavily-cited articles and a long tail of articles cited once, twice, or not at all. The corpus-wide Gini hides substantial sub-cluster variation; comparing per-cluster Ginis is one of the more revealing uses of this tool.

Methodology

Articles are ordered by internal citation count to produce a Lorenz curve; the Gini coefficient is computed as twice the area between the curve and the equality diagonal. A power-law fit (alpha, x_min) is computed via maximum likelihood on the long tail; alternative distributions (lognormal, exponential) are compared via Vuong's likelihood-ratio test.2

Controls

The Lorenz curve and Gini are population statistics — they describe inequality within whatever scope the user chooses. The whole-corpus Gini of 0.909 hides large variation between subfields. Filtering to Composition & Writing Studies or to Technical Communication alone produces different Ginis; comparing per-cluster Ginis is the analytic move.

References

  1. Lotka, A. J. (1926). The frequency distribution of scientific productivity. Journal of the Washington Academy of Sciences, 16(12), 317–323.
  2. Clauset, A., Shalizi, C. R., & Newman, M. E. J. (2009). Power-law distributions in empirical data. SIAM Review, 51(4), 661–703.

For each top-cited article, how concentrated is its audience? Some canonical articles are cited by everyone in their journal; others are cited broadly across journals, decades, and authors. This panel separates the two.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Long Tail asks how concentrated each top-cited article's audience is. Some canonical articles are cited by everyone in their home journal but rarely outside it; others are cited broadly across journals, decades, and authors. The breadth metrics separate the two.

Run with default parameters (top 50 by citation count), Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" leads at 146 citations distributed across 11 unique citing journals and 201 unique citing authors, with a self-journal rate of 38.4% and an author Gini of 0.189 (relatively even citation distribution across citers); its top single citer is Avery C. Edenfield at 4.1% of total citations. The classification scores it as broadly_canonical. Jones's solo 2016 "The Technical Communicator as Advocate" follows at 144 citations across 11 journals and 171 authors with a much lower self-journal rate of 10.4% — the same broad-canonical classification but a different distribution profile, with citation traffic spread more evenly across the field rather than concentrated in TCQ.

Methodology

Per-article metrics: self-journal rate (fraction of citations from the same journal), unique citing-author count, author Gini (concentration of citations among citing authors), top-citer share. Articles are classified broadly_canonical, narrowly_canonical, or mixed via thresholds on those metrics. The classification scheme adapts the diversity-and-concentration framework from interdisciplinarity-of-citation literature.1

Controls

The breadth classification depends on author and journal counts in the citing population. Filtering to a cluster reframes "broad vs. narrow canonical" as broad-or-narrow within that subfield, which is often a more meaningful question than the corpus-wide picture. Top N sets how many top-cited articles to score.

References

  1. Leydesdorff, L., & Rafols, I. (2011). Indicators of the interdisciplinarity of journals: Diversity, centrality, and citations. Journal of Informetrics, 5(1), 87–100.

Top articles, ranked

Articles ranked by citations per year of life rather than raw count. This neutralizes the age advantage older work has and surfaces rapidly-rising contemporary articles. Rank-shift is the difference between an article's raw and time-normalized rank.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Fair Ranking ranks articles by citations per year of life rather than raw count. The normalization neutralizes the age advantage older work has and surfaces rapidly-rising contemporary articles. Each article carries a rank shift: the difference between its raw rank and its time-normalized rank.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus (excluding the most recent two years to stabilize per-year denominators), 85 articles enter the comparison set. Categories sort 15 as stable_canon (articles whose rank stays high under both raw and per-year metrics — established work that still accumulates citations rapidly), 35 as rising_fast (articles that rank far higher per-year than raw, indicating recent trajectory not yet captured by lifetime totals), and 35 as age_advantage (articles whose raw rank substantially exceeds their per-year rank, indicating accumulated rather than active citation). Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" sits at raw rank 1, normalized rank 1, with 14.6 citations per year over 10 years — classified stable_canon: a contemporary article whose ranking holds under both metrics.

Methodology

For an article published in year y, citations-per-year is computed as total internal citations divided by (current year minus y). Articles published in the most recent k years are excluded to avoid noise from very-low-denominator outliers.1 The category assignment compares raw rank against the time-normalized rank: large positive shifts (raw rank much worse than normalized) flag rising_fast; large negative shifts flag age_advantage; small shifts flag stable_canon.

Controls

Exclude last N years drops very-recent publications (their low denominators produce noisy cit/yr values). Bumping it from 2 to 5 produces a more stable contemporary canon at the cost of dropping articles still on the rise. Filtering to a cluster shows who is rising fast within a single subfield.

References

  1. Wang, J. (2013). Citation time window choice for research impact evaluation. Scientometrics, 94(3), 851–872.

Top-cited articles per generation of citing scholars (pre-1995, 1995–2004, 2005–2014, 2015+). Articles can be enduring (top-N every generation), rising (climbing the ranks), fading (slipping off), or generational-only.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Shifting Canons reports the top-cited articles per generation of citing scholars (pre-1995, 1995–2004, 2005–2014, 2015+). Articles can be enduring (top-N every generation), rising (climbing the ranks across generations), fading (slipping off), or generational-only (appearing in only one window).

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, four generations are surfaced. Bruffee's 1984 "Collaborative Learning and the 'Conversation of Mankind'" anchors the pre-1995 generation; Cooper and Selfe's 1990 "Computer Conferences and Learning" leads the 1995–2004 generation; Russell's 1997 "Rethinking Genre in School and Society" leads the 2005–2014 generation. Across the four windows the cross-generation classification flags 7 articles as fading, 5 as rising, 3 as intermittent, and 66 as generational-only — an asymmetry suggesting that most "canonical" articles in any given generation are specific to that era's citing population rather than persisting across decades.

Methodology

For each generation window, the top-N most-cited articles are determined using only citations from articles whose own publication year falls within that window. Cross-generation classification then uses presence and rank movement across the four windows: stable presence with rising rank flags rising; stable presence with falling rank flags fading; non-monotonic presence flags intermittent; presence in only one window flags generational-only.1

Controls

Filtering by cluster rebuilds each generation's top-N from the citation traffic that cluster generates — the comparison shifts to "what each generation of Rhetoric / TPC / CWS scholars treated as canonical." Rising and fading articles surface differently in different clusters, which is a common pattern worth probing.

References

  1. Garfield, E. (1979). Citation indexing: Its theory and application in science, technology, and humanities. Wiley.

By category

The shape of how each top article accumulated its citations: steady classics climb steadily, late bloomers spike years after publication, one-wave articles rise sharply then plateau.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Reach of Citation classifies the shape of each top-cited article's citation accumulation curve. Steady classics climb smoothly across years; late bloomers spike well after publication; one-wave articles rise and decline; front-loaded articles concentrate citations in their first few years.

Run with default parameters across the top 100 articles, the distribution is striking: 78 articles classify as late_bloomer and 22 as steady_classic; the front-loaded and one-wave categories are essentially empty. The dominant pattern in the field is delayed citation accumulation. Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" exemplifies the pattern: 0 citations in 2016 and 2017, then 5 in 2018, 16 in 2019, 8 in 2020, 21 in 2021, peaking at 38 in 2022 (six years after publication), then 14, 19, 22 across 2023–2025. Cumulative citations climbed from 0 to 88 in the first six years and to 146 by 2026. Most top articles in this corpus follow this delayed-recognition shape rather than the immediate-uptake pattern characteristic of fast-moving STEM fields.

Methodology

For each top article, internal citations are bucketed by year of citing article. Pattern classification proceeds via the position and shape of the per-year series:1 steady_classic (smooth growth without a sharp peak), front_loaded (most citations in first 3 years post-publication), late_bloomer (peak occurs more than 5 years post-publication), one_wave (single peak followed by decline), and the residual too_recent / too_few categories.

Controls

Cluster changes both which top-cited articles are eligible (citation count is computed within scope) and which citing articles contribute to each accumulation curve. Top N sets the number of sparkline cards in the grid.

References

  1. Wang, D., Song, C., & Barabási, A.-L. (2013). Quantifying long-term scientific impact. Science, 342(6154), 127–132.

Pattern distribution

Internal (corpus) versus global (OpenAlex) citation rank. Articles that rank high internally but low globally are TPC-specific canon; the reverse pattern (high globally, low internally) signals work the field has imported but not metabolized.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Inside / Outside compares each article's internal (corpus) citation rank against its global (OpenAlex) citation rank. Articles that rank high internally but low globally are field-specific canon; the reverse pattern (high globally, low internally) signals work the field has imported but not metabolized into its own conversation.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the comparison covers 18,620 articles. The Spearman rank correlation between internal and global rank is ρ = 0.57 (p ≈ 0), indicating a moderate but far-from-perfect alignment between the two rankings. The four quadrants partition the corpus: 17,995 articles sit in the background quadrant (low on both), 253 are tpc_specific (high internal, low global), 119 are shared_canon (high on both), and 253 are imported (high global, low internal). The 119 shared-canon articles are the field's strongest contribution to broader scholarly conversation; the 253 imported articles flag work the field reaches for as authority but has not yet integrated into its own dense citation neighborhoods.

Methodology

Internal rank is computed by ordering articles on internal citation count; global rank is computed by ordering on OpenAlex's cited_by_count. The Spearman rank correlation is computed across the full comparison set.1 Articles in the top 100 of either ranking are tabulated; quadrant labels partition the scatter into four interpretive zones using percentile cutoffs on each axis.

Controls

Cluster and journal filters narrow the scatter to articles published in that scope. The Spearman correlation is recomputed on the filtered set, which lets the user ask whether internal and global citation track each other more tightly in TPC than in Rhetoric, or whether one cluster has a heavier "imported" tail than the other.

References

  1. Spearman, C. (1904). The proof and measurement of association between two things. American Journal of Psychology, 15(1), 72–101.

Top divergent articles

Louvain communities computed within four decade windows, then aligned across decades to show how groups split, merge, and persist. Each ribbon traces a community's continuity (or its absence) over time.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Communities through Time runs Louvain community detection within four decade windows (1980s–90s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s) and aligns the partitions across decades to show how research communities split, merge, persist, or disappear. Each Sankey ribbon traces one community's continuity (or its absence) across the windows.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the alignment produces 17 cross-decade ribbons. Rather than a single field-wide community persisting across forty years, the partition shows specialization — communities that emerge in one decade and either stabilize, fragment into smaller clusters in later decades, or disappear when their citation traffic falls below detection threshold. Read the ribbons as a history of how research fronts in writing studies have evolved structurally rather than thematically.

Methodology

For each decade window, an undirected weighted citation graph is constructed and partitioned with NetworkX's Louvain implementation.1 Cross-decade alignment uses shared-article overlap: a community at t+1 is a continuation of a community at t if they share at least 30% of articles (Jaccard-like overlap). Splits, merges, emergences, and extinctions are derived from the alignment.

Controls

Louvain re-runs on whatever edge subset survives the filter. With a tight cluster filter the per-decade community count usually drops (fewer nodes mean a coarser partition); with a broad filter the partition surfaces finer structure. Heavy first-time compute (~30–60s); cached after.

References

  1. Blondel, V. D., Guillaume, J.-L., Lambiotte, R., & Lefebvre, E. (2008). Fast unfolding of communities in large networks. Journal of Statistical Mechanics: Theory and Experiment, 2008(10), P10008.

For each citation community, how much of its citation traffic stays within the community versus crossing to others. Insular communities sit on the y-axis; integrative communities sit toward the diagonal.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Walls and Bridges scores each citation community on how much of its citation traffic stays within the community versus crossing to others. Insular communities sit on the y-axis (high internal density relative to external); integrative communities sit toward the diagonal (citation traffic distributed across community boundaries).

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus (13,442 articles, 44,322 edges, 25 communities), the Cognitive-Process community led by Flower and Hayes's 1981 article carries the highest insularity ratio at 65.1 across 1,185 articles — an extreme case of an inward-facing research front whose internal citation density vastly exceeds its citations to or from neighboring communities. The social-justice TC community led by Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" sits at 25.0 across 1,728 articles — large and still insular, but materially more outward-facing than the Cognitive-Process community. The genre / activity-systems community led by Russell's 1997 "Rethinking Genre" sits at 19.7 across 1,364 articles, which makes it the most integrative of the three largest communities — consistent with Russell's role as the field's heaviest cross-cluster bridge in the Border Crossers tool.

Methodology

Communities come from the same Louvain partition the Explore section's Communities tool uses.1 For each community, internal density is computed as internal edges over (n choose 2); external density is cross-community edges from these nodes over their maximum possible cross-community edges. The insularity ratio is internal density divided by external density. Distribution-based quartiles label communities as insular, moderate, or integrative.

Controls

Filters scope which articles enter the community-detection graph. Insularity classifications are quartile-based within the filtered set — a community that registers as integrative in the whole corpus may register as insular inside its own cluster, which is itself an analytically useful signal.

References

  1. Newman, M. E. J. (2006). Modularity and community structure in networks. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 103(23), 8577–8582.

Per-community detail

Not one main path but several — the few highest-traffic routes through the citation DAG. Where they converge is where the field's intellectual history funnels through a small number of pivotal articles.

Methodology

Tool orientation

First Spark generalizes Main Path Analysis: where Main Path returns the single highest-SPC chain, First Spark extracts the top-K highest-traffic routes through the citation DAG. Where they converge is where the field's intellectual history funnels through a small number of pivotal articles. The global Hummon-Doreian path is included for comparison.

Run with default parameters across the indexed DAG (13,442 nodes, 44,315 edges), the tool returns five top routes. Route 0 is the heaviest at SPC = 1.33 × 1011, an 8-article chain from Kroll's 1985 "Social-Cognitive Ability and Writing Performance" to Winsor's 1999 "Genre and Activity Systems" — the cognition-to-genre-theory pipeline of the 1980s and 1990s. Route 1 (SPC = 9.6 × 1010) is a six-article chain rooted in Spinuzzi's 2010 "Secret Sauce and Snake Oil" terminating at McNely, Spinuzzi, and Teston's 2015 "Contemporary Research Methodologies" — the technical-communication methods chain. Articles linked by shared references — the bibliographic-coupling network of the corpus. Two articles that cite the same prior works are pulled together regardless of whether they cite each other directly.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Shared Foundations renders the bibliographic-coupling network of the corpus: articles linked because they cite the same prior works are pulled together regardless of whether they cite each other directly. Where Citation Network shows who cites whom, Shared Foundations shows who is reading the same things.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus (minimum coupling = 3 shared references), 31,504 article pairs satisfy the threshold; the top 2,000 by weight render as the network with 400 nodes and 2,000 edges. Visible clusters correspond to research fronts whose authors are reading overlapping prior literatures — methodological schools, topical communities, or theoretical lineages whose shared foundations bind them together.

Methodology

For each pair of articles, the count of shared references is computed and weighted by Jaccard similarity to control for very long reference lists.1 Pairs below the minimum threshold are dropped; the top pairs by weight render as a force-directed network. The visible network is therefore a heavily-pruned head of the coupling distribution; the underlying coupling matrix is much larger.

Controls

The coupling network is built from articles in the filtered scope. Cluster reveals what shared-reading patterns exist within a subfield. Min coupling trades graph density for noise — setting it to 5+ focuses the network on the strongest shared-foundation pairs.

References

Two ways to draw the field side-by-side: coupling clusters (what articles read in common) and citation communities (who cites whom). Concordant articles land in matching clusters; discordant articles diverge.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Two Maps draws the field two ways and asks whether they agree. Coupling clusters group articles that read the same prior work; citation communities group articles that cite each other. Concordant articles land in matching clusters under both partitions; discordant articles land in different clusters and mark places where shared foundations diverge from active citation traffic.

Run with default parameters across 2,000 articles, the coupling-side Louvain partition produces 198 clusters; the citation-side partition produces 202 communities; 1,099 articles (54.95%) classify as concordant. The agreement is moderate: NMI = 0.45 and ARI = 0.26, well above random alignment but well below the 0.8–1.0 range that would indicate the two maps tell the same story. The 45% discordance rate is the analytic finding — nearly half of articles sit in shared-foundation neighborhoods that do not coincide with their citation neighborhoods, signaling that what the field is reading and what the field is citing diverge structurally rather than overlap cleanly.

Methodology

Both partitions come from Louvain at default resolution.1 Concordance between partitions is measured by normalized mutual information (NMI) and adjusted Rand index (ARI), which are the standard partition-comparison statistics in the community-detection literature.2 Articles whose coupling-cluster and citation-community labels disagree are flagged discordant.

Controls

Both Louvain partitions re-run on the filtered scope. Selecting a cluster turns the tool into "do these two maps of this subfield tell the same story?" which is often more interpretable than the whole-corpus NMI because the answer can be checked against domain knowledge.

Coupling pairs where the dependence runs much stronger one direction than the other. If A's reference list overlaps heavily with B's, but B's overlap with A is small, A is the dependent in the pair.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Uneven Debts surfaces coupling pairs where the dependence runs much stronger in one direction than the other. If article A's reference list overlaps heavily with B's, but B's overlap with A is small relative to B's own reference list, A is the dependent in the pair: A is reading what B reads, but B is reading much more besides.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, 200 most-asymmetric pairs surface. The maximum asymmetry observed is 0.923 and the median across the top 200 is 0.526. The single most-asymmetric pair is between two articles with shared = 7 references where one carries 91 references and the other carries only 7: the smaller article's reference list is essentially a subset of the larger article's, scoring it as fully dependent (ratio 1.0) on the other (ratio 0.077). Pairs of this shape often surface review-and-derivative relationships, where one article surveys broadly and another draws heavily on a narrow slice of that survey's source corpus.

Methodology

For a pair (A, B) with shared references s, |refs(A)| = ra, |refs(B)| = rb, directional coupling A→B = s/ra; B→A = s/rb; asymmetry = |A→B − B→A|. The dependent article is the one whose ratio is larger.1 The metric directly addresses the long-reference-list bias inherent in symmetric coupling counts.

Controls

Coupling pairs are computed on the filtered scope. Filtering to a single cluster surfaces the most asymmetric debts within that subfield — typically more interpretable than corpus-wide asymmetries because the dependent and the other party share enough disciplinary context for the relationship to make sense.

References

  1. Egghe, L., & Rousseau, R. (2002). Co-citation, bibliographic coupling and a characterization of lattice citation networks. Scientometrics, 55(3), 349–361.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Solo to Squad tracks article team-size over time: the single-author percentage, mean and median author count per decade, and milestone markers for the first 5+-author and 10+-author articles in the corpus. Probable mentor → mentee relationships inferred from publication-year gaps. If A's first publication is 10+ years before B's, and they later co-author, A is treated as a probable mentor of B.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Academic Lineages infers probable mentor → mentee relationships from publication-year gaps. If author A's first indexed publication is 10 or more years before author B's, and the two later co-author at least one article, A is treated as B's probable mentor. The result is a directed mentorship graph that surfaces the field's training relationships as a structural pattern.

Run with default parameters (minimum gap = 10 years), the heuristic identifies 9,346 mentorship pairs across 1,681 distinct mentors. The most prolific mentor by inferred mentees is Richard Leo Enos at 106 mentees, followed by Richard Beach at 82 and Edward M. White at 79 — all senior figures whose long publication histories combined with broad co-authorship rosters produce many qualifying pairs. The single most-active mentorship pair is Cynthia L. Selfe (first pub 1980) and Scott Lloyd DeWitt (first pub 1997) with 23 co-authored articles spanning 2004–2015, a 17-year publication gap that fits the canonical mentor-mentee profile cleanly. The heuristic will mislabel late-career first publications (someone whose first indexed work appears late but whose actual career began earlier) and lateral collaborations between scholars whose careers happen to be offset; the prolific-mentors list is the appropriate filter to read.

Methodology

For each author, first-publication year is computed from the corpus. For each co-author pair, if the gap between their first publications exceeds the threshold (default 10 years), the pair is recorded as a probable mentorship and the older author is recorded as the mentor.1 The reported mentorship graph is therefore a heuristic upper bound rather than a verified network — structural patterns in the graph are interpretable but individual pairs may be mislabeled.

Controls

Filtering to a cluster narrows the corpus the lineage inference runs on, which usually produces a denser local mentorship graph (mentors and mentees are more likely to share a venue). Min year gap tightens the heuristic — 8 catches more pairs with more false positives; 15 catches only obvious mentorships.

  1. Malmgren, R. D., Ottino, J. M., & Nunes Amaral, L. A. (2010). The role of mentorship in protege performance. Nature, 465(7298), 622–626.

Co-authoring pairs classified by persistence. Most pairs are one-shot (a single article); a long tail of pairs span multiple years and articles. The long-running ones are the field's stable collaborative units.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Lasting Partnerships classifies every co-author pair in the corpus by persistence: how long the collaboration ran and how many articles it produced. The bulk of co-authorship is one-shot; a small persistent tail represents the field's stable collaborative units.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, 52,233 co-authoring pairs are classified. The distribution is starkly long-tailed: 48,526 (92.9%) are one_shot (single article), 3,391 (6.5%) are short_term (two articles), and only 316 pairs (0.6%) are persistent (three or more articles spanning at least five years). The most-persistent pair is Joe Erickson and Kevin Roozen at 65 co-authored articles spanning 12 years (2004–2016), followed by Cynthia L. Selfe and Gail E. Hawisher at 58 articles across an extraordinary 28-year span (1988–2016) and Jennifer L. Holberg and Marcy Taylor at 45 articles across 26 years (1999–2025). Long-running partnerships of this scale are rare enough that they constitute a distinct social formation in the field's structure.

Methodology

For each co-author pair, total co-authored articles, year span (last article year minus first article year), and journals involved are computed. Classification: one_shot = 1 article; short_term = 2 articles; persistent = at least 3 articles spanning at least 5 years.1 The boundaries are interpretable rather than empirically optimized; persistence-as-defined captures the field's most-stable collaborative units.

Controls

The pair-classification statistics are computed on the filtered set. Filtering to a cluster surfaces persistent partnerships within that cluster only, which is usually the right question when asking who has long-running collaborative relationships in a particular sub-area.

References

  1. Newman, M. E. J. (2001). The structure of scientific collaboration networks. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 98(2), 404–409.

For each sleeping beauty article, the "princes" that woke it up. Awakening type characterizes how the rediscovery happened: a single catalyst, a new sub-field, or a broad revival.

Methodology

Tool orientation

The Prince Network attaches awakening narratives to each sleeping beauty: which articles cited the beauty during its awakening window, and what pattern those citers form. Awakening type classifies the rediscovery: single_catalyst (one prince paper drives the surge), journal_specific (the awakening concentrates in a single venue), subfield_revival (a single sub-area rediscovers the work), or broad_trend (citations come from across the field).

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the tool returns 15 sleeping beauties with their princes. The dominant pattern is broad_trend at 13 of 15: most awakenings in the indexed corpus are field-wide rather than driven by single catalysts or sub-area revivals. Cargile Cook's 2002 "Layered Literacies" leads the list (the corpus's heaviest sleeping beauty by Beauty Coefficient), with awakening citers spanning multiple TC venues; the 1974 "Students' Right to Their Own Language" introduction shows a similar broad-trend awakening in 2018 driven by the post-2015 social-justice turn. The tool's analytic value lies in distinguishing the structural cause behind each awakening rather than just reporting the awakening event itself.

Methodology

Sleeping beauties come from the existing Beauty Coefficient computation in the Sleeping Beauties tool.1 Princes are the articles that cited the beauty in the awakening window (the years immediately after the awakening year). Awakening types are inferred from the prince set: a single dominant prince flags single_catalyst; concentration in a single journal flags journal_specific; concentration in a single research community flags subfield_revival; broad distribution across the field flags broad_trend.

Controls

Filters narrow the corpus the underlying sleeping-beauty detection runs on. Selecting a cluster reveals subfield-specific awakening patterns — for example, "what kinds of articles wake up the dormant Rhetoric corpus?" Heavy first-time compute when filters change.

References

  1. Ke, Q., Ferrara, E., Radicchi, F., & Flammini, A. (2015). Defining and identifying Sleeping Beauties in science. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 112(24), 7426–7431.
