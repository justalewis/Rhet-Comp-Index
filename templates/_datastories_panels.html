Citation flow between Technical & Professional Communication and Rhetoric/Composition, decade by decade. Each Sankey panel shows where citations originate (left) and where they land (right). The decade summary table beneath quantifies what the eye picks up.

Methodology

Tool orientation

The Braided Path tracks citation traffic between Technical & Professional Communication (TPC) and Rhetoric/Composition (RC) decade by decade. Each Sankey panel shows where the decade's citations originate (left) and where they land (right); width encodes count. The decade summary table beneath quantifies what the eye picks up.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the diagram traces a striking growth and asymmetry. Pre-1990s the field carried 901 inter- and intra-cluster citations — 258 TPC→TPC, 462 RC→RC, 79 TPC→RC, and only 9 RC→TPC. By the 2020s the total has grown twentyfold to 18,250, and the asymmetry persists: TPC cites RC at 862 edges per decade while RC cites TPC at 444. TPC remains the more outwardly engaged neighborhood; RC stays largely inside its own conversation across all five decades.

Methodology

Each citation edge is bucketed by the decade of its citing article. Source and target articles are mapped to a field group via the journal_groups classification (TPC / RC / OTHER). Within each decade, citations are aggregated to TPC→TPC, TPC→RC, RC→TPC, RC→RC, plus OTHER as a residual category. Sankey nodes are per-decade group buckets; widths are raw citation counts. The journal_groups mapping is hand-curated and remains stable across runs.1

Controls

Cluster, journals, and year range apply to both the citing and cited side. Selecting Composition & Writing Studies draws the Sankey using only edges where both endpoints are in CWS journals — useful for asking what intra-cluster citation traffic looks like. Narrowing the year range compares a specific decade against the rest. The decade selector switches which decade pane the diagram displays.

References

  1. Sankey, M. H. P. R. (1898). Introductory note on the thermal efficiency of steam-engines. Minutes of Proceedings of the Institution of Civil Engineers, 134, 278–283.

How the corpus is composed: every indexed journal sorted by tradition. The classification is fixed (the same one all the cross-field tools rely on); this panel shows the resulting balance of articles per group and per journal.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Branching Traditions is the corpus snapshot every cross-field tool depends on. It sorts every indexed journal into one of three traditions and reports the resulting balance of journals and articles per group. Read it as the answer to "what is this index actually composed of?"

Run with default parameters across the full indexed corpus, three traditions emerge. Rhetoric & Composition is the largest at 24 journals and 33,131 articles — 64% of the indexed corpus. Technical & Professional Communication is concentrated and prolific: 9 journals carrying 8,223 articles. The Other / Adjacent bucket — writing centers, WAC, multimodal, international, community-engaged venues — gathers 18 journals and 10,489 articles. The asymmetry between groups (RC has nearly four times as many articles as TPC across more than twice as many journals) is the structural fact behind every cross-cluster citation pattern in the rest of the chapters.

Methodology

The grouping (TPC / RHET_COMP / OTHER) is hand-curated and lives in journal_groups.py. OTHER covers venues whose work is rhetorically and pedagogically adjacent to writing studies but whose primary disciplinary home is elsewhere. Counts come from a single SQL aggregation over the articles table, joined to the journal-group mapping.

Controls

Year range changes which articles get counted toward the per-tradition totals. Narrowing to pre-2000 reads how the index was composed in the field's earlier era; opening to all years gives the corpus snapshot above. The cluster and journal filters are intentionally hidden because this tool is the composition snapshot.

Sources are articles cited inside the corpus but never citing anything in it (foundational works the field reaches back to). Sinks are the reverse: articles that cite into the corpus but are never cited from it (the active research frontier, plus genuine dead-ends).

Methodology

Tool orientation

Origins and Frontiers separates the indexed corpus into the work the field reaches back to (sources: cited but never citing inside the index) and the work that is currently citing into the field but has not yet been cited from it (sinks: citing but never cited). Sinks split into two sub-categories: frontier (recent enough that not being cited is normal) and data-gap (old enough that the absence of citation more likely reflects missing reference deposits than genuine non-citation).

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the tool surfaces 51,843 articles. 41,444 (79.9%) are sources — foundational work that other indexed articles cite but that itself cites nothing inside the corpus, often because its own reference list reaches into books, dissertations, or journals outside the index. 2,998 articles (5.8%) are frontier sinks under the 2021 cutoff, and 5,277 (10.2%) are data-gap sinks — likely victims of reference-deposit coverage rather than genuine isolation. The most-cited source in the field is Adrienne Rich's 1972 "When We Dead Awaken: Writing as Re-Vision" with 402 CrossRef citations — an external-influence text that the indexed corpus reaches back to without the corpus indexing what Rich herself was reading.

Methodology

Sources are articles with internal in-degree ≥ 1 and out-degree = 0; sinks are out-degree ≥ 1 and in-degree = 0. Sinks published after the frontier-cutoff year are flagged frontier; older sinks are data-gap. Per-journal rates report each venue's source-rate and sink-rate, which together describe how the venue contributes to the corpus's intellectual flow.1

Controls

Filtering to a cluster shows what is foundational for that subfield specifically — selecting Technical Communication reveals which articles the TC sub-area reaches back to. The year range shifts the frontier-cutoff implicitly: an upper bound near the present makes more articles look like frontier sinks; a tighter window makes the data-gap category more obvious.

References

  1. Hummon, N. P., & Doreian, P. (1989). Connectivity in a citation network: The development of DNA theory. Social Networks, 11(1), 39–63.

A separate Hummon-Doreian main path computed for each of five decade windows (1970s–80s, 1990s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s). Articles that appear on multiple decades' paths are flagged as persistent; articles that anchor a single decade are decade-specific.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Shifting Currents computes a separate Hummon-Doreian main path for each of five decade windows (1970s–80s, 1990s, 2000s, 2010s, 2020s) and reports each decade's backbone alongside a cross-decade persistence ranking. Articles that appear on multiple decades' paths are flagged as persistent; articles that anchor a single decade are decade-specific.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the five decade paths surface different anchor articles. The 1970s–80s path is short (4 articles) and led by Mike Rose's 1980 "Rigid Rules, Inflexible Plans, and the Stifling of Language." The 1990s extends to 8 articles led by Nancy Nelson Spivey's "Transforming Texts." The 2000s shrinks back to 6 articles anchored by Dorothy Winsor's "Ordering Work." The 2010s explodes to 12 articles led by the multi-author "Symposium: On the Framework for Success in Postsecondary Writing." The 2020s holds 9 articles. The persistence list collects 39 articles that appear on more than one decade's path — a small set whose membership marks the texts that have shaped the field's backbone across multiple eras rather than within a single decade.

Methodology

For each decade, the citation DAG restricted to that decade's articles and their citations is constructed; cycles are removed via topological-sort breaks; SPC (search path count) is computed for every edge; the highest-SPC chain from a source to a sink is reported as the decade's main path.1 Cross-decade persistence is then computed by counting how many decade paths each article appears on. The result is a per-decade reading list plus a persistence ranking that surfaces cross-era anchor texts.

Controls

Filters narrow the DAG that each decade's main path is computed on. Cluster turns the tool into "the decade-by-decade backbone within this subfield." A tight year range makes the per-decade slices sparser; widening usually produces longer paths. Heavy compute on the first cache miss; identical filter combinations return in under a second after.

References

  1. Liu, J. S., & Lu, L. Y. Y. (2012). An integrated approach for main path analysis: Development of the Hirsch index as an example. Journal of the American Society for Information Science and Technology, 63(3), 528–542.

How long it takes citations to land, broken down by direction. TPC→RC is typically slower than TPC→TPC; this panel quantifies that and tracks how delay distributions have shifted across decades.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Speed of Influence quantifies how long it takes citations to land, broken down by source-target field group. Each citation's delay is computed as (citing year − cited year), and the resulting distributions are compared across direction with Mann-Whitney U tests. The question the tool answers is whether different cross-cluster citation directions move at materially different speeds.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, the four directions show distinct tempos. TPC→TPC citations have a median delay of 6 years across 16,102 edges, the fastest direction in the corpus. RC→RC and RC→TPC are both at median 7 years (16,040 and 839 edges respectively). TPC→RC is the slowest at median 8 years across 2,915 edges. The Mann-Whitney comparison between TPC→RC and RC→TPC returns p ≈ 3 × 10−6, indicating a statistically clear delay asymmetry: when TPC reaches across to cite RC, it cites materially older work than when RC reaches across to cite TPC. The TPC→TPC vs. TPC→RC comparison is even stronger (p ≈ 9 × 10−62): citation traffic stays fastest when it stays inside the source cluster.

Methodology

Each citation edge's delay is (citing year − cited year). Edges are bucketed by the field-group of source and target into the four directional categories. Mann-Whitney U tests compare distributions pairwise to flag whether observed differences are statistically distinguishable from random fluctuation.1 Reported statistics include count, mean, median, Q25, Q75, standard deviation, and maximum.

Controls

Filters scope every direction's delay distribution. Applying a cluster filter asks how fast that subfield consumes its own work versus reaches across the boundary. The year range filters by citing year, so narrowing to a recent slice tracks contemporary citation tempo specifically — a useful diagnostic for whether cross-cluster delays are rising or falling over time.

References

  1. Mann, H. B., & Whitney, D. R. (1947). On a test of whether one of two random variables is stochastically larger than the other. Annals of Mathematical Statistics, 18(1), 50–60.

High-betweenness articles that connect TPC and RC neighbourhoods. These are the works that, if removed, would lengthen the average shortest path between the two sub-fields most.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Border Crossers identifies high-betweenness articles that connect TPC and RC neighborhoods. These are the works that, if removed, would lengthen the average shortest path between the two sub-fields most. Border Crossers reports both betweenness scores and a separate count of cross-cluster boundary edges incident to each candidate, so a node ranks high only if it both lies on many shortest paths and actually carries cross-boundary traffic rather than just within-cluster traffic.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus (13,442 articles, 44,322 edges), the top bridge is Jenny Edbauer's 2005 "Unframing Models of Public Distribution: From Rhetorical Situation to Rhetorical Ecologies" (RC, 135 citations, betweenness 0.034, 55 boundary edges), followed by Bruffee's 1984 "Collaborative Learning and the 'Conversation of Mankind'" (RC, 108 citations, 61 boundary edges) and Russell's 1997 "Rethinking Genre in School and Society" (RC, 129 citations, 93 boundary edges) — the article with the highest count of TPC-RC boundary edges in the entire corpus. Jones, Moore, and Walton's 2016 "Disrupting the Past" appears at fourth place but with only 16 boundary edges, indicating that its high citation count and within-cluster centrality do not translate to a heavy cross-cluster connector role: it is central to TPC, not bridging TPC to RC.

Methodology

NetworkX's exact betweenness centrality is computed on the full undirected citation graph using Brandes's algorithm.1 Boundary-crossing flow (the count of cross-cluster edges incident to each node) is reported alongside betweenness so the user can distinguish genuine bridges from locally-central nodes. The neighborhood subgraph for each top bridge is included for the force-directed view, which renders the bridge node together with its first-degree cross-cluster neighbors.

Controls

Bridge detection runs on whatever subgraph the filters carve out. Filtering to a cluster finds within-cluster bridges — useful when "TPC-vs-RC" is not the boundary of interest. Year range on citing date constrains which edges the betweenness algorithm sees. Heavy first-time compute (60–90s) per unique filter combination; cached after.

References

  1. Brandes, U. (2001). A faster algorithm for betweenness centrality. Journal of Mathematical Sociology, 25(2), 163–177.

Bridge neighbourhood

When TPC cites RC, does RC cite back? The reciprocity rate at the article level reveals how much intellectual traffic actually runs both ways and which TPC articles attract the most return citation.

Methodology

Tool orientation

Two-Way Street asks whether cross-cluster citation runs both ways: when a TPC article cites RC, does any RC article cite back? The reciprocity rate at the article level reveals how much intellectual traffic actually runs both ways and which articles attract the most return citation.

Run with default parameters across the indexed corpus, RC reciprocates with TPC at a substantially higher rate than the reverse. Of 1,277 TPC articles that cite RC work, only 270 (21.1%) get cited back by RC; of 387 RC articles that cite TPC work, 143 (37.0%) get cited back by TPC. The asymmetry runs against intuition: even though TPC reaches out to cite RC more often than the reverse (1,277 outgoing vs. 387), RC is the more reliably reciprocating cluster when it does reach out. At the strict edge level (A→B paired with a literal B→A counterpart), the rate is 0.0% because article-pair reciprocity through the same two articles is rare; the analytic move is at the article-level reciprocity, where the question is whether each cluster's outreach gets any return engagement at all.

Methodology

Article-level reciprocity for an article A is the count of distinct articles in the other group that A both cites and is cited by. Edge-level reciprocity is the fraction of A→B edges with a B→A counterpart on the same article pair. Trends are computed per decade of the citing article so the user can read whether reciprocity rates are rising or falling over time.1

Controls

Cluster and journal filters scope which articles get counted on each side of the boundary. A useful pattern is to lock to Rhetoric, run, then switch to Technical Communication — the comparison is whether one cluster reciprocates more than the other. Year-range filters narrow the analysis to a specific decade, which can surface generational shifts in cross-cluster engagement.

References

  1. Wasserman, S., & Faust, K. (1994). Social network analysis: Methods and applications. Cambridge University Press.

Most-reciprocated articles

The Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient for the corpus's citation distribution, plus a power-law fit. The visual answer to "how unequal is citation in this field?"

Methodology

Articles ordered by internal citation count produce a Lorenz curve; Gini = 2 × the area between the curve and the equality diagonal. A power-law fit (alpha, x_min) is computed via maximum likelihood on the long tail; alternative distributions (lognormal, exponential) are compared.

Controls

The Lorenz curve and Gini are population statistics — they tell you how unequal citation is within whatever scope you choose. A whole-corpus Gini ~0.91 hides large variation between subfields. Filter to Composition & Writing Studies alone and the Gini may shift; comparing per-cluster Ginis is one of the more revealing ways to use this tool.

For each top-cited article, how concentrated is its audience? Some canonical articles are cited by everyone in their journal; others are cited broadly across journals, decades, and authors. This panel separates the two.

Methodology

Per-article metrics: self-journal rate (fraction of citations from the same journal), unique citing-author count, author Gini (concentration of citations among citing authors), top-citer share. Articles are classified broadly_canonical / narrowly_canonical / mixed via thresholds on those metrics.

Controls

The breadth classification depends on author / journal counts in the citing population. Filtering to a cluster reframes "broad vs narrow canonical" as broad-or-narrow within that subfield, which is often a more meaningful question than the corpus-wide picture. Top N sets how many top-cited articles to score.

Top articles, ranked

Articles ranked by citations per year of life rather than raw count. This neutralizes the age advantage older work has and surfaces rapidly-rising contemporary articles. Rank-shift is the difference between an article's raw and time-normalized rank.

Methodology

For an article published in year y, citations-per-year = total internal citations ÷ (current year − y). Articles published in the most recent k years are excluded by default to avoid noise from very-low-denominator outliers. Categories: stable_canon, age_advantage, rising_fast.

Controls

Exclude last N years drops very-recent publications (low denominator results in noisy cit/yr). Bumping it from 2 to 5 produces a more stable contemporary canon at the cost of dropping articles still on the rise. Filter to a cluster to see who's rising fast within a single subfield.

Top rising-fast vs. age-advantage

Top-cited articles per generation of citing scholars (pre-1995, 1995–2004, 2005–2014, 2015+). Articles can be enduring (top-N every generation), rising (climbing the ranks), fading (slipping off), or generational-only.

Methodology

For each generation window, the top-N most-cited articles are determined using only citations from articles whose own publication year falls within that window. Cross-generation classification uses presence and rank movement across the four windows.

Controls

Filtering by cluster rebuilds each generation's top-N from the citation traffic that cluster generates — the comparison is "what each generation of [Rhetoric / TPC / CWS] scholars treated as canonical." Rising and fading articles surface differently in different clusters, which is a common pattern worth probing.

By category

The shape of how each top article accumulated its citations: steady classics climb steadily, late bloomers spike years after publication, one-wave articles rise sharply then plateau.

Methodology

For each top article, citations are bucketed by year. Pattern classification: steady_classic (smooth growth), front_loaded (most citations in first 3 years), late_bloomer (peak more than 5 years post-publication), one_wave (single peak then decline), too_recent, too_few.

Controls

Cluster changes both which top-cited articles are eligible (citation count is computed within scope) and which citing articles contribute to each accumulation curve. Top N sets the number of sparkline cards in the grid.

Pattern distribution

Internal (corpus) versus global (OpenAlex) citation rank. Articles that rank high internally but low globally are TPC-specific canon; the reverse pattern (high globally, low internally) signals work the field has imported but not metabolized.

Methodology

Internal rank: rank by internal citation count. Global rank: rank by OpenAlex's cited_by_count. Spearman rank correlation between the two is reported. Articles in the top 100 of either ranking are tabulated; quadrant labels split the scatter into four interpretive zones.

Controls

Cluster and journal filters narrow the scatter to articles published in that scope. The Spearman rank correlation reported in the summary is recomputed on the filtered set — a useful comparison axis is "how well does internal citation track global citation in [TPC] vs in [Rhetoric]?"

Top divergent articles

Louvain communities computed within four decade windows, then aligned across decades to show how groups split, merge, and persist. Each ribbon traces a community's continuity (or its absence) over time.

Methodology

For each decade window, an undirected weighted citation graph is partitioned with NetworkX's Louvain implementation. Cross-decade alignment uses shared-article overlap: a community at t+1 is a continuation of a community at t if they share at least 30% of articles (Jaccard-like). Splits, merges, emergences, and extinctions are derived from the alignment.

Controls

Louvain re-runs on whatever edge subset survives the filter. With a tight cluster filter the per-decade community count usually drops (fewer nodes mean a coarser partition); with a broad filter you see finer structure. Heavy first-time compute (~30-60s).

For each citation community, how much of its citation traffic stays within the community versus crossing to others. Insular communities sit on the y-axis; integrative communities sit toward the diagonal.

Methodology

Communities come from the same Louvain partition the Explore section's Communities tool uses. For each community: internal density = (internal edges) / (n choose 2), external density = (cross-community edges from these nodes) / (their max possible). Insularity ratio = internal / external. Distribution-based quartiles label communities insular / moderate / integrative.

Controls

Filters scope which articles enter the community-detection graph. Insularity classifications (insular / moderate / integrative) are quartile-based within the filtered set — a community that's "integrative" in the whole corpus may register as "insular" inside its own cluster, which is a useful signal.

Per-community detail

Not one main path but several — the few highest-traffic routes through the citation DAG. Where they converge is where the field's intellectual history funnels through a small number of pivotal articles.

Methodology

SPC (search path count) is computed for every edge in the global citation DAG. The top-K edges by SPC are extracted; their connected components yield key routes. Convergence points are articles that sit on more than one route. The global Hummon-Doreian path is included for comparison.

Controls

Heaviest tool in this section. Filters narrow the DAG; the SPC computation then traces top routes through that subgraph. Filtering to a single cluster typically yields shorter routes (fewer cross-cluster traversals inflate path counts) but with sharper convergence points. Cached after first compute per unique filter combination.

Articles linked by shared references — the bibliographic-coupling network of the corpus. Two articles that cite the same prior works are pulled together regardless of whether they cite each other directly.

Methodology

For each pair of articles, count shared references; weight by Jaccard similarity to control for very high-reference articles. Pairs below the minimum threshold are dropped; the top pairs by weight render as a force-directed network.

Controls

The coupling network is built from articles in the filtered scope. Cluster reveals what shared-reading patterns exist within a subfield. Min coupling trades graph density for noise — set to 5+ to focus on the strongest shared-foundation pairs.

Two ways to draw the field side-by-side: coupling clusters (what articles read in common) and citation communities (who cites whom). Concordant articles land in matching clusters; discordant articles diverge.

Methodology

Both partitions come from Louvain at default resolution. Concordance is measured with normalized mutual information (NMI) and adjusted Rand index (ARI). Articles whose coupling-cluster and citation-community labels disagree are flagged discordant.

Controls

Both Louvain partitions (coupling-side and citation-side) re-run on the filtered scope. Picking a cluster turns the tool into "do these two maps of [this subfield] tell the same story?" which is often more interpretable than the whole-field NMI.

A "master works" list derived from the Datastories book's own figures: which articles appear across the most chapters' analyses. The closer an article gets to appearing in every chapter, the more central it is to the book's argument.

Methodology

This tool aggregates the article ID lists every other Datastories tool returns. An article's score is the count of distinct tools (and chapters) it appears in. Tied articles are broken by total internal citation count.

Controls

The aggregate is built from filter-aware sub-tools. Apply a cluster to derive a master-works list specific to that subfield — articles that show up across that cluster's view of every other tool. Each underlying tool uses the same filter, so the answer is "books everyone in [scope] reads, by Datastories' own measures."

Coupling pairs where the dependence runs much stronger one direction than the other. If A's reference list overlaps heavily with B's, but B's overlap with A is small, A is the dependent in the pair.

Methodology

For a pair (A, B) with shared references s, |refs(A)| = ra, |refs(B)| = rb: directional coupling A→B = s/ra; B→A = s/rb; asymmetry = |A→B − B→A|. Dependent article = the one whose ratio is larger.

Controls

Coupling pairs are computed on the filtered scope. Filtering to a single cluster surfaces the most asymmetric debts within that subfield — typically more interpretable than corpus-wide asymmetries because the dependent and other party share enough disciplinary context for the relationship to make sense.

Article team-size over time. The single-author percentage, the median author count, and the year the median crossed each threshold — with milestone markers for the first 5+ author article and the first 10+ author article.

Methodology

Author counts come from splitting the authors field on the corpus's standard delimiter and counting non-empty segments. Yearly aggregates are means, medians, and 75th-percentile team size; milestones are derived programmatically.

Controls

Filters constrain which articles' authorship counts get tabulated. Apply a cluster to compare team-size trajectories across subfields: TPC and CWS often look quite different, with technical communication trending toward larger teams earlier. Year range truncates the timeline.

Largest teams in the corpus

Probable mentor → mentee relationships inferred from publication-year gaps. If A's first publication is 10+ years before B's, and they later co-author, A is treated as a probable mentor of B.

Methodology

For each author, first-publication year is computed from the corpus. For each co-author pair, if the gap exceeds the threshold (default 10 years), the pair is recorded as a probable mentorship. This is a heuristic and will mislabel late-career first publications and lateral collaborations.

Controls

Filtering to a cluster narrows the corpus the lineage inference runs on, which usually produces a denser local mentorship graph (mentors and mentees are more likely to share a venue). Min year gap tightens the heuristic — 8 catches more pairs with more false positives; 15 catches only obvious mentorships.

Most-prolific mentors

Co-authoring pairs classified by persistence. Most pairs are one-shot (a single article); a long tail of pairs span multiple years and articles. The long-running ones are the field's stable collaborative units.

Methodology

For each co-author pair: total co-authored articles, year span, journals involved. Classification: one_shot = 1 article; short_term = 2 articles; persistent = ≥3 articles spanning ≥5 years.

Controls

The pair-classification statistics are computed on the filtered set. Filtering to a cluster surfaces persistent partnerships within that cluster only, which is usually what you want when reading "who has the long-running collaborative relationships in TPC?"

Persistent partnerships

For each sleeping beauty article, the "princes" that woke it up. Awakening type characterizes how the rediscovery happened: a single catalyst, a new sub-field, or a broad revival.

Methodology

Sleeping beauties come from the existing Beauty Coefficient computation. Princes are the articles that cited the beauty in the awakening window. Awakening types: single_catalyst, individual_rediscovery, journal_specific, subfield_revival, broad_trend.

Controls

Filters narrow the corpus the underlying sleeping-beauty detection runs on. Picking a cluster reveals subfield-specific awakening patterns — e.g. "what kinds of articles wake up the dormant Rhetoric corpus?" Heavy first-time compute when filters change.

A curated timeline of disciplinary events (journal foundings, landmark articles, special issues, external crises) laid against the corpus's awakening spikes. Where the two coincide, an event is plausibly causal.

Methodology

The events table is hand-curated. Awakening spikes come from per-year counts of sleeping-beauty awakening years. Correlations are post-event 5-year window means, by event type.

Controls

The events list itself is curated and not filtered. Cluster and year range reshape the awakening-spike series the events are laid against; with a cluster filter the post-event awakening rates by event type often shift, suggesting that some event types catalyse some subfields more than others.

Articles cited heavily in the wider scholarly world (per OpenAlex) but rarely cited inside this corpus. The "unread canon" is what the field has imported via author awareness but hasn't woven into its own citation fabric.

Methodology

Filter to corpus articles where global cited_by ≥ 100 and internal cited_by ≤ 2. Rank by the ratio (global / max(1, internal)). Authors of these articles tend to be field-adjacent figures whose work is gestured at but not built upon.

Controls

Filters scope which articles count as "unread canon." A cluster filter answers a different question: "what does [Rhetoric] cite from the wider scholarly world but not metabolize internally?" — frequently more interesting than the corpus-wide list.
